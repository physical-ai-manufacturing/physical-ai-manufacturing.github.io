OVERVIEW001

The Two Gaps

Robot learning has made significant progress in recent years, with demonstrations of folding laundry, stacking dishes, and pouring coffee. Despite these impressive demonstrations, robot learning has been slow to adopt in manufacturing, a domain with trillion-dollar economic stakes. Two gaps explain why:

GAP 01FAILURE MODE

The Capability Gap

The tasks our field has most visibly demonstrated are ones a human can learn in minutes. The tasks that drive value in manufacturing are skilled tasks that take humans months to years to master, such as polishing a turbine blade, stacking battery cells into a pack, laying a clean weld bead on a curved seam, or seating a connector into a sub-millimeter tolerance. These require precise, compliant, and contact-rich control over long horizons, and they remain largely out of reach for learned systems.

Skilled tasks out of reach
Turbine-blade polishing Battery-cell pack stacking Curved-seam weld bead Sub-mm connector seating
GAP 02FAILURE MODE

The Reliability Gap

Traditional automation already dominates a regime of repeatable tasks with minimal variation in high volume. Dedicated cells run one task millions of times with decades of uptime. The opening for robot learning lies in tasks which frequently vary, where skilled human labor is still required. To capture this regime, a system is needed that runs 24/7, hits cycle-time targets, and fails predictably when it fails at all. We have shown that learned policies can attempt many tasks. We have not shown that skilled tasks in a meaningful mix can be executed to a production-grade standard, every time.

What “reliable” must mean
Cycle-time consistency Mean time between failures Failure severity & recovery High-mix, low-volume throughput

In this workshop, we aim to bring together researchers and industry practitioners to study both gaps. We will examine what actually causes learned systems to fail in deployment, how to measure reliability in ways that are both academically rigorous and operationally meaningful (cycle time, mean time between failure, failure severity, recovery behavior), and what current methods lack when faced with the precision, contact richness, and long horizons that skilled manufacturing requires. We also recognize that part of why these gaps persist is fundamentally due to the fact that skilled-manufacturing data is largely proprietary, and contact-rich long-horizon benchmarks are scarce. As part of this workshop, we will explore the following questions:

TOPICS002

Six Guiding Questions

Q.01

What separates skilled tasks like polishing, welding, and tight-tolerance insertion from the tasks robot learning has most successfully demonstrated, and what would it take for learned methods to close that gap?

Q.02

What does reliability actually mean for a learned robotic system, and how should we measure it beyond task success rate?

Q.03

What is the right division of labor between learning and classical control, planning, and sensing for precise, long-horizon manufacturing?

Q.04

What does it take to reliably handle the mix of a high-mix, low-volume cell, rather than one task in isolation or many tasks only once?

Q.05

Why do today's methods plateau so far below the reliability bar production demands and what are their dominant failure modes?

Q.06

How can academic benchmarks align with the bar set by real production lines, in both capability and reliability?
